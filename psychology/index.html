Before Any Technique

The one idea that changes everything

The psychologist is not scoring your sentence, they are reconstructing the person who wrote it.

WAT, TAT, SRT and the Self-Description are four camera angles on one personality. The assessor's real job is to assemble a single, consistent picture of who you are, and to check that the same person shows up across the psychology desk, the GTO ground, and the interview chair.

The consequence most coaching misses: a polished, "perfect" answer that contradicts the rest of your profile is worse than an average honest one. A candidate who owns a weakness and shows they're working on it reads as far more credible than one who claims to have none. Manufactured positivity is the number-one giveaway, trained assessors spot pretence instantly.

The Real Objective

We are not teaching tricks. We are training the reflex until your honest answer and the officer-like answer become the same answer. When that happens, the timer stops being an enemy and becomes proof that you're genuine.

Consistency is the whole game

WAT reveals your instinctive value-reflex. SRT reveals your instinctive action-reflex. They must agree with each other, with your TAT stories, and with the person who walks into the interview room.

The four-week training rhythm

WeekFocus
Week 115 WAT words/day + 20 SRTs/day, untimed. Build honesty and connect words to real experiences.
Week 2Introduce the timer. 30 WAT + 30 SRTs/day. Practise the "blank → dash → move on" SOP.
Week 3Full sets: 60 WAT + 45–55 SRTs. Sort every SRT by dilemma type; hunt for double-binds.
Week 4Mock conditions + peer/mentor review. Audit for consistency across WAT, SRT and self-description.
