Test Two · Deep Dive

Competing priorities — hold both, sacrifice neither

What's really tested: The trap is that it presents as a choice — family OR meeting, exam OR friend in trouble, your event OR your health. The immature candidate treats it as a fork and picks one, abandoning the other. The assessor is watching whether you're resourceful enough not to fully drop either responsibility. (Same skill as the double-bind escape.)

The mechanism — delegation + partial coverage

You almost never have to be physically present in two places, but you can almost always keep both fronts covered. Attend to the higher-priority front personally, and delegate or arrange coverage for the other with clear instructions.

Signature example — family emergency vs. important meeting

"He briefs his deputy to run the meeting with clear notes, leaves for the family emergency, and follows up on the meeting outcome later."

Prioritises correctly (family emergency is genuinely higher) but does NOT abandon the meeting — hands it off properly and closes the loop. Four qualities: judgment, delegation, planning, responsibility.

The three ways to answer

ResponseVerdict
Abandon front A ("rushes to family, forgets the meeting")Fails — dropped a responsibility entirely.
Abandon front B, the human one ("attends the meeting first, it's important")Fails — cold, wrong priority, no heart.
Hold both (prioritise + delegate + follow up)Passes.
The priority rule

When the two fronts are genuinely unequal, life/health/family emergencies generally outrank scheduled commitments — but the professional one is covered, not cancelled. When they're closer in weight, sequence them (do the short urgent one, then the other) or compress (a quick call now, full attention later). The answer is never "which one do I sacrifice" — it's "how do I keep both alive."

More worked one-liners
  • Family emergency vs. exam: "He informs the examiner/authority, arranges for a family member to reach the hospital, sits the exam, and goes straight after" — or, if the emergency is severe, reverses the order and applies for a re-exam through proper process. Neither is silently dropped.
  • Event to lead vs. sudden illness: "He hands charge to his second-in-command with a clear brief, and supports remotely as far as his health allows."
  • Two commitments at the same hour: "He attends the one only he can handle, delegates the other to a capable teammate, and checks the outcome afterward."

The through-line in every case: prioritise honestly → delegate or arrange coverage → close the loop.
