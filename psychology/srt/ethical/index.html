Test Two · Deep Dive

Ethical dilemmas — acting through the proper channel

What's really tested: integrity without recklessness. Can you refuse to ignore wrongdoing and refuse to blow it up yourself?

What "proper channel" actually means

Every organisation — the Army, a school, a company — has a defined chain of responsibility. "Acting through the proper channel" means you route the problem to the person or body whose job it is to deal with it, following that chain, rather than either (a) staying silent or (b) taking it into your own hands publicly.

  • Colleague in unethical practice: first, a direct word if it's minor and correctable ("I noticed this, you should fix it"); if it's serious or continues, escalate to your immediate superior or the relevant authority (reporting officer, ethics/vigilance cell, head of department). Document what you saw. Don't announce it to the whole office, and don't cover for them.
  • Senior officer taking a bribe: you can't confront a senior the way you'd confront a peer, and you can't ignore it. Report it to the appropriate higher authority or designated body through the correct ladder — with facts, not gossip, and not to the media.

The three failure modes (only one passes)

ResponseVerdict
Ignoring it ("I'd mind my own work")Fails on integrity — you'll look away from wrongdoing.
Grandstanding / vigilante ("I'd expose him in front of everyone" / "I'd fight him myself")Fails on judgment — recklessness, ego, no sense of process.
Through proper channelPasses — you act, but through the correct authority, calmly, with facts.
How to write it in one line — the formula

[verify quietly] → [act at the right level] → [escalate with facts if needed].

Worked examples
  • Bribe: "He notes the facts, reports the matter to the appropriate higher authority through proper channel, and lets the process take its course."
  • Colleague: "He first advises his colleague to stop; if it continues, he informs the concerned superior with the facts."

Two refinements

  • Match the level to the severity. A colleague rounding off a form gets a quiet word; a colleague forging records gets escalated. A junior's mistake you handle yourself; a senior's corruption you route upward. Not using a sledgehammer for everything is itself a sign of judgment.
  • Act on facts, not suspicion. "He verifies the facts" before acting signals maturity — you're not accusing on a hunch.
